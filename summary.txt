ASSIGNMENT 3 — TINY TRANSFORMER: SUMMARY
=========================================

TASK
----
Build a Transformer language model from scratch in PyTorch, trained on the Tiny Shakespeare
dataset for next-token prediction. Primary metric: validation perplexity (PPL = exp(val loss)).


DATASET
-------
- Corpus: Tiny Shakespeare (~1M characters)
- Tokenizer: Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) via HuggingFace `tokenizers`, vocab_size=500
- Actual vocab size after training: 500 tokens (including [UNK] and [PAD] special tokens)
- Total tokens after encoding: ~447,717
- Sequence format: sliding window of length SEQ_LEN=50, stride=1
  - Input:  tokens[i : i+50]
  - Target: tokens[i+1 : i+51]
- Total sequences: ~447,667
- Split: 80% train (358,133 sequences) / 20% val (89,534 sequences)
- Batch size: 64


MODEL ARCHITECTURE
------------------
Name: TinyTransformer
Total parameters: ~658K (varies slightly due to weight tying)

Components (in order):
1. Token embedding: nn.Embedding(500, 128)
2. Sinusoidal positional encoding (precomputed buffer, shape [1, T, 128])
   - PE(pos, 2i)   = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/128))
   - PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/128))
3. Dropout(0.1) on the summed embeddings
4. 2x Transformer blocks, each with:
   a. Pre-norm: RMSNorm(128)
   b. Causal multi-head self-attention (4 heads, d_k=32 per head)
      - Q, K, V projections (no bias)
      - Scaled dot-product: softmax(QK^T / sqrt(32)) * V
      - Causal mask: upper-triangular -inf before softmax
      - Returns attention weights for visualization
   c. Residual connection: x = x + attn_out
   d. Pre-norm: RMSNorm(128)
   e. Feed-forward: Linear(128→256) → GELU → Dropout → Linear(256→128) → Dropout
   f. Residual connection: x = x + ffn_out
5. Final RMSNorm(128)
6. LM head: Linear(128→500, no bias), weight-tied to token embedding

RMSNorm implementation:
  rms = sqrt(mean(x^2) + eps)
  out = scale * x / rms   (scale is a learned parameter, init=1)

Weight initialization: Normal(mean=0, std=0.02) for all Linear and Embedding layers.


TRAINING SETUP
--------------
- Loss: CrossEntropyLoss (logits reshaped to [B*T, vocab_size], targets to [B*T])
- Optimizer: AdamW (lr varies by experiment, weight_decay=0.01)
- LR schedule: Cosine annealing over total training steps
- Gradient clipping: max norm = 1.0
- Epochs: 20 per experiment
- Best checkpoint saved whenever val loss improves


EXPERIMENTS & RESULTS
---------------------
Three learning rate configurations were compared. All other hyperparameters held constant.

baseline    | 3e-4  |    52.62     |    18      |    52.66
lr_high     | 1e-3  |    52.00     |     9      |    52.63

Key observations:
- lr_high (1e-3) converges fastest — reaches competitive PPL by epoch 9, best overall at 52.00.
  After epoch 9, val loss plateaus/slightly oscillates while train loss keeps dropping,
  suggesting mild overfitting or the cosine schedule killing effective learning too early.
- baseline (3e-4) is stable and steady — val loss decreases smoothly through epoch 18,
  converging to 52.62. Very close to lr_high's final PPL despite slower start.
- lr_low (1e-4) underperforms — still descending at epoch 20 (PPL 57.46, never plateaued),

DESIGN DECISIONS & JUSTIFICATIONS
-----------------------------------
- RMSNorm over LayerNorm: required by assignment; also simpler and empirically comparable.
- Pre-norm architecture: more stable training than post-norm at small scale.
- Weight tying (embedding ↔ LM head): reduces parameters and regularizes the output projection.
- GELU activation: smoother gradient than ReLU, standard in modern Transformers.
- Cosine LR schedule: prevents aggressive late-training updates; complements AdamW.
- Gradient clipping (norm=1.0): stabilizes training, especially at lr_high.
- Sinusoidal positional encoding: deterministic, no learned parameters, generalizes to
  unseen lengths. Required by assignment.
- Stride=1 sliding window: maximizes training signal from the corpus at the cost of
  higher memory usage (mitigated by large val/train sequence counts).


POTENTIAL IMPROVEMENTS
----------------------
The train/val gap (~3.33 vs ~3.96 at epoch 20) indicates overfitting as the primary bottleneck
— the model memorizes more than it generalizes. Bigger or longer training is not the fix;
better regularization is.

1. Label smoothing (highest impact, trivial to add)
   - CrossEntropyLoss(label_smoothing=0.1)
   - Prevents the model from becoming overconfident on training tokens, directly penalizing
     the behavior driving the train/val gap.

2. More dropout (0.2 or 0.3 instead of 0.1)
   - Train loss keeps falling while val stalls — a classic sign that dropout is too light.
   - Apply to attention weights and FFN activations.

3. LR warmup before cosine decay
   - Currently cosine annealing starts from the full LR at step 0.
   - A short linear warmup (~500 steps) stabilizes early training and often leads to a
     better loss basin before the decay phase begins.

4. Larger sliding window stride (stride=5 or 10 instead of 1)
   - Stride=1 means adjacent training sequences share 49/50 tokens — extremely correlated.
   - A larger stride produces more diverse batches, acting as a natural regularizer and
     reducing the effective dataset redundancy.
